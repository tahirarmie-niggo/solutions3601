Choosing between them

Three questions decide this, not the brochure

01

What can your family actually show?

Every embassy wants proof of funds, and the figure differs sharply. Germany asks for a blocked account of around €11,900; the UK wants tuition plus maintenance held for 28 days. This rules countries in or out faster than anything else.

02

Do you want to stay after graduating?

If the answer is yes, Canada and Australia are built for it. If you plan to return to Pakistan with a recognised degree, cheaper EU options make far more sense.

03

Does your subject have an entry test?

Medicine in Italy requires IMAT. Some Polish medical faculties set their own exam. These have fixed dates, so they decide your timeline rather than the other way round.

Before you pick one

Questions we get about destinations

Can I apply to more than one country at once?

Yes, and for most students we recommend it. Applications are cheap compared to the cost of losing an intake, so we usually file into two countries with different funding requirements. You only commit once an offer and a visa are in hand.

Which country is cheapest overall?

Germany on tuition, but the blocked account means you need a large sum available upfront. Poland and Hungary usually work out lowest on total cost once living expenses are counted, particularly for medicine. The cheapest tuition is rarely the cheapest year.

Does a refusal from one country affect the others?

It can. Most visa forms ask whether you have been refused elsewhere, and answering dishonestly is far more damaging than the refusal itself. We declare it and address the original ground directly in the new application.

I want permanent residency eventually. Where should I look?

Canada and Australia have the clearest study-to-residency pathways, and both are designed around it. European routes exist but are slower and more conditional. We will be straight with you about which is realistic for your profile rather than selling the idea.

Do I need to speak the local language?

Not for the English-taught programmes we place into, which covers everything listed above. German and Italian public universities do run local-language tracks that are cheaper, but those need a language qualification before you can apply.
